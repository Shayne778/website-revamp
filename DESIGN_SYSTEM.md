# Design System: Local Business Website Transformation Mockups

## Purpose

This design system guides the creation of modern homepage mockups for local businesses with outdated websites.

The goal is not to create generic templates. The goal is to transform each business's existing information, photos, branding, and service details into a cleaner, more trustworthy, mobile-first website concept.

Each design should feel like a polished evolution of the current business, not an unrelated rebrand.

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Preserve the Business Identity

Use the existing website as the source material.

Preserve when appropriate:

- Business name
- Logo
- Brand colors
- Service categories
- Photos
- Local positioning
- Company history
- Existing calls to action
- Trust signals

Improve:

- Layout
- Visual hierarchy
- Mobile usability
- Copy structure
- Calls to action
- Service clarity
- Contact flow
- Use of images
- Professional polish

### 2. Modernize Without Misrepresenting

The redesigned mockup should make the business look better, but it must not invent facts.

Do not create fake:

- Reviews
- Certifications
- Licenses
- Awards
- Guarantees
- Before-and-after outcomes
- Medical claims
- Legal claims
- Years in business
- Team members
- Service areas

If a section needs missing information, use neutral placeholder copy or mark it as needing confirmation.

### 3. Design for Lead Generation

Most local business websites should drive one of these actions:

- Phone call
- Quote request
- Appointment request
- Service booking
- Contact form submission
- Direction request

The design should make the next step obvious.

## General Visual Style

The default style should be:

- Clean
- Modern
- Trustworthy
- Local
- Professional
- Mobile-first
- Fast to understand
- Conversion-focused

Avoid:

- Overly artistic layouts
- Gimmicky animations
- Dense text blocks
- Tiny typography
- Weak contrast
- Generic stock-photo feel
- Excessive gradients
- Fake corporate polish that does not match the business

## Homepage Structure

Most mockups should include:

1. Header/navigation
2. Hero section
3. Trust or reputation strip
4. Services section
5. Why choose us section
6. Gallery or project-photo section
7. About section
8. Service-area section
9. Frequently asked questions, if useful
10. Contact or quote-request section
11. Footer

## Header

Recommended elements:

- Logo
- Business name if logo is weak or unclear
- Navigation links
- Phone number
- Primary call-to-action button

Mobile header:

- Logo or business name
- Tap-to-call button
- Menu button

Avoid clutter. The header should make contacting the business easy.

## Hero Section

The hero section should immediately answer:

- What does this business do?
- Where do they serve?
- Why should the visitor trust them?
- What should the visitor do next?

Hero content:

- Clear headline
- Short subheadline
- Primary call-to-action
- Secondary call-to-action
- Strong image if available
- Trust signal if verified

Example structure:

```text
[Business Name] provides [service] for homeowners in [city/region].
Clear, reliable service with an easier way to request a quote.
[Request a Quote] [Call Now]
```

Do not use exaggerated claims like "best," "number one," or "guaranteed" unless the existing site clearly supports them.

## Service Cards

Service cards should make the business easy to understand quickly.

Each card should include:

- Service name
- One to two sentence description
- Optional image or icon
- Optional link or call-to-action

Use real services from the old website.

Do not invent services.

## Trust Section

Use verified trust signals only.

Possible trust signals:

- Years in business, if stated
- Licensed, if stated
- Insured, if stated
- Family-owned, if stated
- Locally owned, if stated
- Google rating, if confirmed
- Testimonials from the current site, if already published there
- Certifications from the current site
- Awards from the current site

Avoid fake review cards. If real testimonials are not available, use a general reputation section without fabricated quotes.

## Gallery or Project Section

Use existing website assets where possible.

Good uses:

- Project photos
- Service photos
- Team photos
- Before-and-after photos already published by the business
- Equipment or worksite photos

If image quality is poor:

- Use fewer images
- Crop carefully
- Place images in cards
- Use them lower on the page instead of as the hero
- Consider a neutral design with image placeholders

Do not use unrelated stock images unless clearly marked as placeholders.

## About Section

The about section should transform old long-form copy into concise, trust-building copy.

Include:

- Who the business serves
- What services they provide
- What makes them credible
- Local connection
- Owner-operated or family-owned status, if stated

Avoid excessive biography unless it helps sell trust.

## Service-Area Section

For local businesses, include a clear service-area block if the old site mentions locations.

Examples:

- Fort Myers
- Cape Coral
- Naples
- Bonita Springs
- Estero
- Punta Gorda
- Port Charlotte

Do not invent service areas.

If service areas are unclear, say:

> Serving [region] and surrounding communities.

Only use that wording if it is reasonable based on the business location and service type.

## Contact and Quote Section

The contact section should be simple and conversion-focused.

Recommended fields:

- Name
- Phone
- Email
- Service needed
- Message

For home services, consider:

- Property location
- Preferred contact method
- Urgency

For appointment-based businesses, consider:

- Preferred date
- Preferred time
- Service type

Include:

- Phone number
- Address, if available
- Hours, if available
- Service-area note

## Footer

The footer should include:

- Business name
- Phone number
- Address
- Email
- Navigation links
- Services
- Service areas
- Disclaimer if mockup is private concept only

For private mockups, include a subtle internal note outside the visible business design if needed:

> Private concept mockup for demonstration purposes only.

## Industry Style Guidance

### Pool Service and Pool Cage Repair

Visual direction:

- Clean blue, aqua, white, navy
- Bright outdoor images
- Water, patio, pool surface, screened enclosure visuals
- Clean cards and rounded sections

Tone:

- Reliable
- Clean
- Responsive
- Local

Useful sections:

- Weekly pool service
- Repairs
- Pool cage repair
- Equipment checks
- Green pool cleanup
- Service areas
- Quote request

### Roofing

Visual direction:

- Charcoal, white, muted red, slate
- Strong contrast
- Durable, professional feel
- Large project images if available

Tone:

- Protective
- Experienced
- Straightforward
- Trustworthy

Useful sections:

- Roof repair
- Roof replacement
- Storm damage
- Inspections
- Emergency service, only if actually offered
- Financing, only if actually offered

### Landscaping and Hardscaping

Visual direction:

- Green, cream, stone, charcoal
- Outdoor imagery
- Project gallery emphasis
- Before-and-after presentation if available

Tone:

- Professional
- Clean
- Visual
- Local

Useful sections:

- Lawn care
- Landscape design
- Hardscaping
- Irrigation
- Maintenance
- Gallery

### Hurricane Windows and Shutters

Visual direction:

- Navy, white, gray, coastal accents
- Safety and storm-readiness emphasis
- Clean architectural visuals

Tone:

- Protective
- Practical
- Trustworthy
- Florida-specific

Useful sections:

- Impact windows
- Hurricane shutters
- Installation
- Free estimates
- Storm protection
- Service areas

### Med Spa and Aesthetics

Visual direction:

- White, beige, soft gold, charcoal
- Calm spacing
- Premium but not exaggerated
- Careful use of imagery

Tone:

- Professional
- Trust-focused
- Conservative with claims
- Clean and calm

Important:

Do not add unsupported clinical claims, guaranteed outcomes, fake before-and-after images, or fake testimonials.

## Copywriting Rules

Use plain, conversion-focused language.

Good copy:

- Clear
- Specific
- Local
- Service-oriented
- Trust-building
- Easy to scan

Avoid:

- Buzzwords
- Overpromising
- Generic filler
- Unsupported superlatives
- Fake urgency
- Medical or legal claims without support

Better:

> Request a quote for pool service in Cape Coral.

Worse:

> Experience the ultimate pool transformation from the number one experts in Florida.

## Mobile-First Requirements

Every mockup must work well on mobile.

Mobile priorities:

- Clear headline above the fold
- Tap-to-call button
- Large readable text
- Simple navigation
- No dense text blocks
- Fast access to quote form
- Sticky call or quote button when appropriate
- Images cropped for small screens

## Accessibility Basics

Use:

- High color contrast
- Readable font sizes
- Descriptive button text
- Proper heading hierarchy
- Alt text for images when known
- Large tap targets
- Forms with clear labels

## Asset Use Rules

Use assets from the business's existing website only as source material for the private concept mockup.

Keep a record of:

- Asset file name
- Source page
- Original URL
- Intended use
- Any uncertainty about ownership or quality

Do not mix in third-party assets without marking them as placeholders.

## Mockup Deliverable Standard

A good mockup should:

- Look substantially better than the current site
- Use the business's real information
- Use available photos and branding where appropriate
- Make the business easier to understand
- Make contacting the business easier
- Feel plausible as the next version of their actual website
- Be simple enough to build quickly
- Be polished enough to use in outreach

## Final Rule

Do not design a fake business.

Transform the real business's existing website into a better version of itself.
